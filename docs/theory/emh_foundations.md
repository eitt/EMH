# EMH Foundations and Latin American Market Context

## Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)
The Efficient Market Hypothesis, formulated by Eugene Fama in 1970, posits that financial markets are "informationally efficient." This means that asset prices fully reflect all available information.

### Forms of EMH:
1.  **Weak-form Efficiency**: Prices reflect all information contained in past trading data (prices, volumes). Technical analysis cannot generate excess returns.
2.  **Semi-strong form**: Prices reflect all public information (earnings reports, news).
3.  **Strong form**: Prices reflect even private/insider information.

## Latin American Markets
Emerging markets, particularly in Latin America, often exhibit different efficiency characteristics compared to developed markets (e.g., US or EU).

### Contextual Factors:
- **Liquidity Frictions**: Lower trading volumes and higher bid-ask spreads can lead to slower information incorporation.
- **Institutional Quality**: Regulatory frameworks and market transparency vary across the region.
- **Regime Volatility**: Frequent macroeconomic shifts (indexed inflation, currency devaluations) create "non-stationary" price signals that may be predictable using advanced ML.

## Predictability vs. Efficiency
A central challenge in this research is distinguishing between **predictability** (the ability of a model like Diffusion to find patterns) and **statistical arbitrage**. Markets may be predictable but not profitable after transaction costs, which is often termed "adaptive efficiency."
